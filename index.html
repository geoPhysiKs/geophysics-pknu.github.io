Who We Are

trying to know what seismograms tell

We study the sources of large and small earthquakes occurring at home and abroad, including the 2016 ML 5.8 Gyeongju earthquake and 2017 ML 5.4 Pohang earthquake. Using the seismic observation data obtained by directly operating a high-density broadband seismic observation network, the characteristics of the seismic sources associated with these earthquakes, such as the range and posture of the underground fault motion that caused the Gyeongju and Pohang earthquakes, could be revealed in great detail.
We welcome students interested in seismological research!

What We Do

trying to find and dig seismograms

We are very interested in using seismic waves to reveal the crust and mantle structures of East Asia including the Korean Peninsula, but not limited to the area. Cross-correlation analysis of the background noise of continuous seismic records recorded from the seismic network of the southern Korean Peninsula and the seismic network of neighboring China and Japan, and the seismic wave tomography method were applied to the detailed crust structure of the lower part of the Korean Peninsula and the seismic wave velocity structure of the crust and upper mantle of the lower part of East Asia was imaged.

Where We Go

trying to get where seismograms are

Modern seismology is observational science. Our lab is leading research to further investigate the characteristics of earthquake sources and the internal structure of the Earth through extensive and detailed earthquake observations. We operate high-performance seismic observation equipment and real-time and precise analysis computer servers, and conduct larger and more detailed research in cooperation with related seismology research groups at home and abroad. The ultimate goal of these studies is to create a society that is safe from earthquake disasters, starting with a fundamental search for natural phenomena.
